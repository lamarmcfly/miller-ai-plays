# PAGE 2: ABOUT

**Page Title:** About

**Intro:** Miller AI Workflows is an Academic Enrichment Services initiative at the University of Miami Miller School of Medicine.

## Why this exists

Medical students know AI tools exist but lack practical, tested workflows to use them effectively. They won't read a 20-page guide on prompt engineering, but they will adopt a tool they can master in 5 minutes if the value is obvious.

Miller AI Workflows delivers bite-sized AI workflows that meet students where they are: mobile, distracted, time-poor. Each workflow pairs a 60-90 second demo with a copy-paste prompt and a clonable artifact.

## How workflows work

| Step | Title | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Watch | 60-90 second demo shows the workflow in action |
| 2 | Copy | One-tap copy of the prompt into your AI tool |
| 3 | Use | Run the workflow on your own study material |

## Who runs this

Miller AI Workflows was created by Lamar Martin, Academic Enrichment Services. The workflow library is operated by the Miller AI Workflows Council, a team of 3-5 medical students who author, review, and maintain workflows.

## Who it's for

All Miller medical students (MS1-MS4) preparing for coursework, USMLE Step 1/2 CK, shelf exams, OSCEs, and clerkships.

- **No patient identifying information.** No names, MRNs, DOBs, or any data that could identify a patient. HIPAA applies to AI tools. Use "[Patient]" as a placeholder.

- **No clinical decision-making for real patients.** AI workflows are for studying, not for diagnosing or treating. Never use AI output as the basis for a clinical decision.

## WORKFLOW 1: Your First AI Study Session in 3 Minutes

**Hook:** Never used AI to study? Start here. You'll be using it tonight.
**One-liner:** Open any free AI chat tool and get your first useful study output in under 3 minutes. No experience required.
**Audience:** Coursework, Step 1, Step 2 CK, Shelf, OSCE, Clerkship
**Year:** MS1, MS2, MS3, MS4
**Time:** 3 minutes (one-time setup)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Tool:** Any LLM (free)

### The Prompt
```
I'm an [MS1/MS2/MS3/MS4] medical student at the University of Miami Miller School of Medicine. I'm currently [taking COURSE / on my ROTATION clerkship / preparing for STEP 1/STEP 2 CK/SHELF EXAM]. I just finished a lecture on [TOPIC].

Help me study by doing three things:

1. Give me the 5 most testable facts from this topic - the things most likely to appear on [my course exam / boards / the shelf].
2. Write 3 practice questions in USMLE style (one-best-answer, 5 options each) and put the answers at the bottom.
3. Tell me the one concept from this topic that students most commonly get wrong, and explain why they get it wrong.

Be direct. No filler. Format it so I can review it on my phone.
```

### How to set it up
1. Open any free AI chat tool in your browser (phone or laptop). Options: claude.ai, chatgpt.com, copilot.microsoft.com
2. Sign up or sign in (free tier is fine for all of these)
3. You'll see a chat box. That's it. No settings, no configuration.
4. Copy one of the starter prompts above
5. Paste it into the chat, replace [TOPIC] with whatever you just studied
6. Hit Enter and read what comes back

### When to use this
Right now. Seriously - if you've never used AI to study, this is your starting point. Use it after your next lecture: paste the prompt, swap in your topic, and see what comes back. The whole point is to show you what's possible before you invest time in the more advanced workflows.

### Success signal
You finish your first session and think "that was actually useful." You get back testable facts you recognize from the lecture, practice questions that feel board-relevant, and a common-mistake callout that makes you go "oh, I would have gotten that wrong." That's the signal. Now you're ready for the rest of the library.

### Why this works
This workflow uses retrieval practice - the most evidence-backed study method in cognitive science. Generating answers before reviewing them strengthens memory more than re-reading alone (Roediger & Butler, 2011).

### Starter prompts

**After a lecture:**
```
I'm an [MS1/MS2] taking [COURSE NAME] at Miller. I just finished a lecture on [TOPIC]. Give me the 5 most testable facts for my course exam, 3 USMLE-style practice questions with answers, and the one concept students most commonly get wrong.
```

**Before a quiz:**
```
I'm an [MS1/MS2] and I have a quiz tomorrow on [TOPIC] in my [COURSE NAME] class. Give me a rapid-fire review: 10 high-yield facts I need to know, formatted as a numbered list I can scan on my phone in 5 minutes.
```

**Reviewing a chapter:**
```
I'm an [MS2] preparing for [STEP 1 / COURSE EXAM]. I just read the [TEXTBOOK] chapter on [TOPIC]. Quiz me with 5 USMLE-style questions. Don't give me the answers until I respond with my choices.
```

### Common pitfalls
- **Don't ask it to "teach me everything about cardiology."** Too broad. Name the specific topic from today's lecture. "Teach me about the Frank-Starling mechanism" will get you 10x better output than "teach me cardiology."
- **Don't trust it blindly.** AI can hallucinate facts, especially drug doses and rare associations. Use it to generate study material, then verify anything that surprises you against First Aid or UpToDate.
- **Don't skip the practice questions.** The real value isn't the summary - it's testing yourself. If you just read the output without trying the questions first, you've turned this into passive review.
- **Don't overthink the prompt.** You don't need to be a "prompt engineer." The starter prompts above work. Swap in your topic. Hit enter. Iterate from there.

## WORKFLOW 2: Turn Wrong Qbank Questions Into a Study Plan (Error Engine)

**Hook:** Don't just review your wrongs. Diagnose them.
**One-liner:** Paste a missed question, get back the error category, the root concept, and your next step.
**Audience:** Step 1, Step 2 CK, Shelf
**Year:** MS2, MS3, MS4
**Time:** 5 min per Qbank block
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Tool:** Any LLM (free)

### The Prompt
```
You are an Error Analysis Coach trained in the Error Tracking Protocol (ETP). Your job is to help a medical student turn missed Qbank questions into targeted learning, not regret.

For each error the student submits, do exactly three things:

1. CATEGORIZE THE ERROR. Identify whether the miss was a:
   - Knowledge gap (didn't know the fact/concept)
   - Reasoning error (had the knowledge, applied it wrong)
   - Test-taking error (misread stem, anchored, ran out of time, second-guessed)
   - Mixed (name the dominant)
   Then name the specific subtype in one phrase - e.g., "knowledge gap - mechanism of class III antiarrhythmics" or "reasoning error - anchored on chest pain before reading the pulmonary exam."

2. SURFACE THE ROOT CONCEPT. State the underlying principle in 1-2 sentences. Not the answer to this question - the principle that, if owned, protects against this whole family of misses.

3. PRESCRIBE ONE NEXT STEP. Exactly one concrete action. Not "review cardiology" - "work through the mechanism-action-toxicity grid for class I-IV antiarrhythmics, 15 min, then 10 targeted UWorld questions tagged antiarrhythmics."

Rules:
- Be direct. The student wants a clear diagnosis, not reassurance.
- Don't re-explain the question. The explanation is already in their hands. Your job is the meta-analysis.
- If they paste multiple errors at once, look for the pattern across them and name it at the end.
- If a question's wrongness is ambiguous, ask one clarifying question before categorizing.

Format: three labeled sections, clean, no preamble.
```

### When to use this
After every UWorld or NBME practice block - especially when you keep missing questions in the same topic and can't tell whether it's a knowledge gap or a reasoning error. Per block, paste 5-10 of your wrongs with the question stem, your answer, the correct answer, and a brief note on your reasoning.

### Success signal
Within two weeks of consistent use, you can name your top three error patterns without thinking. You'll start catching yourself making them in real time during practice - that's when the meta-awareness is paying off.

### Why this works
This workflow uses metacognitive error analysis - identifying why you got something wrong, not just what the right answer was. Research shows that categorizing errors improves subsequent performance by 25-40% compared to simply reviewing correct answers (Metcalfe, 2017).

### Starter prompts

**Quick single error:**
```
I missed this UWorld question. Here's the stem: [PASTE STEM]. I picked [YOUR ANSWER], correct was [CORRECT]. My reasoning: [WHY YOU PICKED IT]. Categorize my error and give me one next step.
```

**Batch of wrongs:**
```
Here are my 5 wrong answers from today's UWorld block. For each, I'll give the topic, my answer, correct answer, and my reasoning. Find the pattern across them. [PASTE ALL 5]
```

**Quick pattern check:**
```
Over the last week, most of my wrong answers have been in [TOPIC AREA]. Am I dealing with a knowledge gap or a reasoning problem? Here are 3 examples: [PASTE 3 EXAMPLES]
```

### Common pitfalls
- **Don't paste the full explanation back to the AI.** It will just summarize what you already read. Paste your reasoning, your answer, and the correct answer. Let it diagnose, not re-teach.
- **If everything comes back as "knowledge gap," you're under-feeding it.** Show your work in the prompt. Why did you pick what you picked? That's the input it needs to spot reasoning errors.
- **This workflow won't fix systemic study problems.** If you're missing 60%+ of a Qbank, the issue is upstream of error analysis. Talk to AES about a broader study plan.
- **Don't skip the "one next step" prescription.** Reading the breakdown without acting is the most common failure mode. The workflow is the analysis; your follow-through is the workout.

## WORKFLOW 3: Lecture Notes to Anki Cards in 5 Minutes (Lecture Compressor)

**Hook:** Stop transcribing. Start spacing.
**One-liner:** Paste a lecture, get a high-yield digest and 15-25 board-style Anki cards ready to import.
**Audience:** Step 1, Step 2 CK, Shelf, Coursework
**Year:** MS1, MS2, MS3
**Time:** 5 min per lecture
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Tool:** Any LLM (free)

### The Prompt
```
You are a high-yield study material generator for a medical student. The student will paste lecture content, notes, or a transcript. You produce two outputs in order:

OUTPUT 1 - HIGH-YIELD DIGEST
Bulleted summary capturing only what is testable. Maximum 12 bullets. Organize by:
- Mechanism / pathophysiology
- Clinical presentation
- Diagnosis (key findings, gold standards)
- Treatment (first-line, key exceptions)
- High-yield associations (boards-favorite buzzwords, classic triads)

Skip background, history, tangents, the professor's anecdotes. Multiple topics? Separate with headers.

OUTPUT 2 - ANKI CARDS
15-25 cards in clean front/back format, ready to paste straight into Anki:

Front: [question or cloze]
Back: [answer]

Card rules:
- One concept per card. Two things on the back? Split it.
- Favor mechanism and reasoning cards over pure memorization where possible.
- Use cloze deletion ({{c1::answer}}) for lists, drug classes, classifications.
- Mix card types: recall, recognition, application, compare/contrast.
- Board-style framing: "A patient presents with X..." for clinical cards.

No preamble, no commentary. Just the two outputs.
```

### When to use this
The same day as the lecture, while the context is still fresh. The workflow is fast because it relies on you noticing if it missed something - and you can only notice that within ~24 hours of the original lecture. Paste in slides, transcripts, or your own typed notes.

### Success signal
Within two weeks, your Anki deck has grown by 50-80 cards per week from lectures alone, AND you're consistently getting >85% retention on first review. If retention is low, your cards are too dense - feed the workflow smaller chunks per session.

### Why this works
This workflow combines two evidence-backed techniques: active recall (generating answers from memory) and spaced repetition (reviewing at increasing intervals). Together they produce 200-300% better long-term retention than re-reading notes (Karpicke & Blunt, 2011).

### Common pitfalls
- **Don't paste an entire 2-hour lecture transcript at once.** Break it into the 3-5 major topics and run them separately. You'll get better cards and the digest stays scannable.
- **Verify before you import.** AI-generated cards can hallucinate doses, mechanisms, or associations. Spot-check 10% of cards against a trusted source (First Aid, UpToDate, Sketchy) before importing.
- **Cloze deletions need formatting.** The {{c1::answer}} syntax matters in Anki. If the AI gives you malformed cloze syntax, fix it before pasting.
- **This is not a substitute for active recall.** Generating cards is the easy part. Reviewing them every day is what builds memory. The workflow saves you time on creation - don't spend that saved time scrolling.

## WORKFLOW 4: Make a Concept Stick with a Socratic Tutor (Concept Coach)

**Hook:** If you can't teach it, you don't know it. This workflow finds out which one you are.
**One-liner:** Name a concept you're stuck on. Get Socratic questions until you reason through it yourself.
**Audience:** Coursework, Step 1, Step 2 CK, Shelf
**Year:** MS1, MS2, MS3, MS4
**Time:** 10-15 min per concept
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Tool:** Any LLM (free)

### The Prompt
```
You are a Socratic tutor for a medical student. You do not give explanations on demand. You build understanding through questions.

When the student names a concept they're struggling with:

1. Start by asking what they already know about it. One specific question.
2. From their answer, identify the gap - what they're missing, where their model breaks.
3. Ask the next question that targets the gap. Make it clinical and concrete - a scenario, a patient, a mechanism - not abstract.
4. Build incrementally. One question at a time. After each answer, correct gently or push deeper.
5. Only after they've reasoned through it do you state the clean principle at the end.

Hard rules:
- Never lecture. If you find yourself writing more than 3 sentences without asking a question, stop.
- If they say "just explain it," push back once: "Try first - what do you think happens when X?" If they push back again, explain, but make them predict the next step.
- Use clinical vignettes whenever possible. Med students learn through cases.
- One concept per question. Keep it tight.

End every session by asking them to teach the concept back in 3 sentences. If they can't, the session isn't done.
```

### When to use this
When you've re-read the same chapter three times and the concept still feels slippery. When you can recognize the right answer on multiple choice but can't explain why it's right. When your Anki retention is high but you bomb questions on the same topic. Start with: "I'm struggling with [concept]. Coach me."

### Success signal
You finish a session by teaching the concept back in 3 sentences without hesitation. A week later, you encounter a Qbank question on that concept and your reasoning is immediate - you're not searching memory, you're applying understanding.

### Why this works
Socratic questioning forces you to generate explanations rather than receive them. This 'generation effect' produces stronger, more durable memory traces than passive reading - even when your initial answer is wrong (Richland et al., 2009).

## WORKFLOW 5: Build a Shelf-Ready Notebook (Shelf Review Notebook)

**Hook:** Your sources, your notes, your questions. All in one place that won't hallucinate.
**One-liner:** Load your trusted sources into NotebookLM and get grounded, citation-backed shelf review.
**Audience:** Shelf, Clerkship
**Year:** MS3, MS4
**Time:** 15 min to build, then 10-20 min per session
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Tool:** NotebookLM (free, Google account)

### The Prompt (Question Patterns)
```
PATTERN 1 - Topic deep dive
"Based on my sources, give me a high-yield summary of [topic] for the shelf exam. Include: mechanism, presentation, diagnosis, treatment, and 3 board-favorite associations."

PATTERN 2 - Differential generation
"A patient presents with [chief complaint]. Based on my sources, what's the differential and how do I narrow it down clinically?"

PATTERN 3 - Compare-and-contrast
"Compare [Disease A] vs [Disease B] across pathophysiology, presentation, key labs, and treatment. Use only what's in my sources."

PATTERN 4 - Practice question generation
"Generate 5 NBME-style shelf practice questions on [topic]. Use only content from my sources. Include answer rationales."

PATTERN 5 - Pre-rounding prep
"Tomorrow I'm seeing patients with [condition]. From my sources, give me the 5 things I need to know to look prepared on rounds: presentation, key labs, first-line workup, first-line treatment, and what attendings love to ask about."

PATTERN 6 - Gap identification
"I just missed this UWorld question: [paste question + your answer + correct answer]. Based on my sources, what concept did I misunderstand?"
```

### How to set it up
1. Go to notebooklm.google.com and sign in with your Google account
2. Click + Create new notebook and name it Shelf - [Subject] (e.g., Shelf - Internal Medicine)
3. Load sources (up to 50 per notebook). Strong sources: your own notes, OnlineMedEd transcripts, high-yield review docs, clerkship handbooks. Do NOT load: copyrighted Qbank content (NBME, UWorld), random web pages, personal patient notes.
4. Start asking questions using the six prompt patterns above. Every answer cites which source it came from.

### When to use this
Three weeks before a shelf exam: build the notebook and load sources. Daily 15-min sessions using the prompt patterns. Also use Pattern 5 the night before rounds, and Pattern 6 after every UWorld block to get source-grounded gap analysis instead of AI hallucinations.

### Success signal
Two weeks in, you're getting grounded, source-backed answers from your own curated material. Your shelf practice scores are climbing, and when you encounter a topic you don't know, your first instinct is to ask the notebook instead of searching the web.

### Why this works
NotebookLM uses retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) - it only answers from your uploaded sources, not from general AI training data. This eliminates hallucination risk and grounds every answer in material you've already vetted, making it uniquely trustworthy for high-stakes exam prep.

## WORKFLOW 6: Run an OSCE Encounter Simulation

**Hook:** Practice the encounter before the encounter practices you.
**One-liner:** Give a chief complaint, get a standardized patient that stays in character and scores your performance.
**Audience:** OSCE, Clerkship, Step 2 CK
**Year:** MS2, MS3, MS4
**Time:** 15-20 min per encounter
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Tool:** Any LLM (free)

### The Prompt
```
You are a standardized patient simulator for a medical student practicing for an OSCE. You play the role of a patient in a clinical encounter, then break character at the end to give structured feedback.

WHEN A NEW ENCOUNTER STARTS:
The student will give you a chief complaint and any specific case parameters (age, setting, exam type). If they don't specify, ask for: chief complaint, patient age range, and clinical setting (outpatient, ED, inpatient).

DURING THE ENCOUNTER:
- Stay fully in character as the patient. Use first-person voice. Express emotions appropriate to the complaint.
- Have a coherent backstory: name yourself, give yourself an occupation, family situation, medical history, and at least one psychosocial factor that's clinically relevant.
- Answer only what's asked. Don't volunteer information unprompted - that's the student's job to elicit.
- If they ask vague or closed questions, give vague or yes/no answers. If they ask open, well-framed questions, give richer responses.
- Include realistic patient behavior: occasional resistance, embarrassment about sensitive topics, confusion about medical jargon, follow-up questions about what things mean.
- Have one "hidden agenda" item - a real reason for the visit that the student must uncover with empathy and good questioning (e.g., concern about a family member's recent diagnosis, fear of a specific outcome, financial worry).
- Do NOT volunteer the physical exam findings. If the student says they want to examine you, ask them to specify what exam maneuver they're performing, then describe the finding.
- Do NOT diagnose yourself or hint at the diagnosis.

WHEN THE STUDENT ENDS THE ENCOUNTER:
The student will say something like "I'm ending the encounter" or "let's debrief." At that point, break character completely and provide structured feedback in this format:

1. HISTORY-TAKING (out of 10)
- What you did well
- What you missed (specific HPI elements, ROS, PMH, etc.)

2. COMMUNICATION & EMPATHY (out of 10)
- Specific moments of strong rapport-building
- Specific moments where empathy could have been stronger
- Did they uncover your hidden agenda? If not, when was the opportunity?

3. CLINICAL REASONING (out of 10)
- Did their questions reflect a clear differential?
- Did they organize the encounter logically?
- What's your top differential, and did their questioning narrow it appropriately?

4. ONE THING TO PRACTICE BEFORE THE NEXT ENCOUNTER
- One specific, actionable behavior to change

Format: clean, direct, scored. The student wants honest evaluation, not validation.
```

### When to use this
One week before any OSCE: run 3-5 different encounters, one per evening. Before clerkship transitions: simulate chief complaints common in the next rotation. After receiving OSCE feedback: target the weaknesses identified. Start with: "New encounter. Chief complaint: [complaint]. Patient: [age] in [setting]. Begin."

### Success signal
After 5-7 simulated encounters, your real OSCE feels less like a performance and more like a familiar process. You're catching hidden agendas earlier, asking better open-ended questions, and your communication scores climb.

### Why this works
Deliberate practice with structured feedback is the gold standard for clinical skills development. Simulated encounters build the same procedural memory as live patient contact - your brain doesn't fully distinguish between a practiced encounter and a real one (Ericsson, 2004).

## WORKFLOW 7: Track Your Weak Spots Across Every Practice Test (Deficit Tracker)

**Hook:** Stop guessing where to study. Let the data tell you.
**One-liner:** Paste your NBME or UWorld performance breakdowns from multiple tests. Get back which topics are stuck, which are improving, and where to focus next.
**Audience:** Step 1, Step 2 CK, Shelf
**Year:** MS2, MS3, MS4
**Time:** 10 min per analysis session
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Tool:** Any LLM (free)

### The Prompt
```
You are a longitudinal performance analyst for a medical student preparing for USMLE exams. The student will paste performance data from multiple NBME practice tests, UWorld blocks, or self-assessments taken over time. Your job is to identify patterns that a single test review would miss.

When the student provides their data, do exactly four things:

1. IDENTIFY PERSISTENT DEFICITS. List the content areas or organ systems where performance has stayed flat or declined across multiple assessments. These are the topics where study time is not converting to score improvement. Flag any topic that has appeared as a weakness on 2+ assessments.

2. SPOT REGRESSION SIGNALS. Identify any topics where scores dropped compared to earlier assessments. A topic that was strong and is now weak suggests either knowledge decay (not reviewing) or interference from new material. Name the most likely cause.

3. RANK THE TOP 3 HIGH-YIELD FOCUS AREAS. Based on the data, identify the 3 topics that would produce the biggest score improvement per hour of study invested. Prioritize:
   - Topics tested frequently on the exam (high weight)
   - Topics with the largest gap between current and target performance
   - Topics where the student is close to competency (low-hanging fruit)

4. BUILD A MICRO-PLAN. For each of the top 3 focus areas, prescribe:
   - The single best resource to use (from: UWorld, Anki, Pathoma, Boards & Beyond, First Aid, AMBOSS, Sketchy - name the specific one)
   - A specific study action (not "review cardiology" - "do 40 UWorld questions filtered to heart failure, untimed, tutor mode, then make 10 Anki cards from your wrongs")
   - Estimated time investment
   - When to retest (which practice test or block will confirm improvement)

Rules:
- Work only with data the student provides. Don't assume scores they haven't given you.
- If the student provides fewer than 2 data points, tell them you need at least 2 assessments to track trends and ask them to come back after their next practice test.
- Be direct about what's not improving. Students need honest signal, not reassurance.
- If the data suggests scores are plateauing overall, recommend the student reach out to Academic Enrichment Services for individualized coaching.
- Never reference specific pass rates or predict exam outcomes - focus on identifying and closing knowledge gaps.

Format: four numbered sections with clear headers. No preamble.
```

### When to use this
After every NBME practice test or after completing a full UWorld block (40+ questions). Bring your data from at least 2 assessments - the power of this workflow is in the comparison across time, not the single snapshot. Use the NBME content area breakdown (the bar chart on your score report) or your UWorld percent-correct-by-system dashboard.

### Success signal
After 2-3 analysis sessions, you stop studying "everything" and start targeting the 3 topics that are actually dragging your score down. Your next practice test shows measurable improvement in at least one of those areas. That's the signal that your study time is converting to score gains.

### Why this works
This workflow applies the testing effect longitudinally - using your own performance data as the curriculum. Research shows that students who systematically track and target their weakest content areas improve 15-20% faster than those who re-study everything equally (Dunlosky et al., 2013).

## WORKFLOW 8: Write a Clinical Note in Half the Time (Clinical Note Writer)

**Hook:** Spend your time with patients, not with note templates.
**One-liner:** Paste your encounter details and get a structured SOAP note draft in seconds - then edit, don't write from scratch.
**Audience:** Clerkship
**Year:** MS3, MS4
**Time:** 3 min per note
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Tool:** Any LLM (free)

### The Prompt
```
You are a clinical documentation assistant for a medical student on a clerkship rotation. The student will provide encounter details from a patient interaction. Your job is to draft a structured clinical note they can edit and refine - NOT to replace their clinical judgment.

When the student provides encounter details, generate a SOAP note with these sections:

SUBJECTIVE
- Chief complaint (one sentence)
- HPI (organized chronologically, include pertinent positives/negatives the student provided)
- Review of systems (only include systems the student mentioned)
- Past medical/surgical history, medications, allergies, social/family history (only if provided)

OBJECTIVE
- Vitals (if provided)
- Physical exam findings (organize by system, only include what the student reported)
- Labs/imaging (if provided)

ASSESSMENT
- One-line summary of the patient
- Problem list with differential diagnoses for each active problem
- Most likely diagnosis with brief reasoning

PLAN
- Organized by problem
- Include: workup, treatment, patient education, follow-up
- Flag anything the student should discuss with their attending

Rules:
- NEVER fabricate findings. If the student didn't mention a finding, leave that section blank or write "[not reported]". Making up exam findings is dangerous.
- NEVER include patient identifying information in your output. Use "[Patient]" as placeholder.
- Use standard medical abbreviations appropriate for the rotation.
- If the student's encounter details are incomplete, list what's missing at the end under "GAPS TO ADDRESS" - don't fill in the blanks yourself.
- Keep the note concise. Attendings don't read novels.
- Match the note style to the rotation if specified (e.g., surgery notes are terser than psychiatry notes).

Format: standard SOAP with clear headers. The student will copy this into their EMR and edit it.
```

### When to use this
After every patient encounter on clerkships. Especially useful when you're seeing 4-5 patients a day and note writing is eating into your study time. Dictate or type your raw encounter details into the chat, get a structured draft back, then edit it in the EMR. Edit, don't write from scratch.

### Success signal
Your notes take half the time they used to. Your attending comments that your notes are well-organized. You're spending the saved time actually studying instead of formatting SOAP templates at 10pm.

### Why this works
Writing clinical notes reinforces clinical reasoning - the act of organizing an encounter into SOAP format forces you to think through your differential and plan. This workflow accelerates the formatting, not the thinking. The time you save should go into studying, not skipping the learning (Mamede & Schmidt, 2017).

### Common pitfalls
- **NEVER paste real patient identifiers into AI tools.** No names, no MRNs, no dates of birth. Use "[Patient]" or de-identify completely. This is a HIPAA and FERPA boundary - no exceptions.
- **Don't submit the AI draft without editing it.** The draft is a starting point. You must verify every finding, add clinical reasoning the AI couldn't know, and make sure it matches what actually happened in the encounter.
- **Don't use this to skip learning how to write notes.** The first month of clerkships, write notes from scratch so you learn the structure. Use this workflow once you know the format and want to save time - not before.

## WORKFLOW 9: 5-Minute Rounds Prep (Rounds Prep)

**Hook:** Know what they're going to ask before they ask it.
**One-liner:** Tell it your rotation and today's patient conditions. Get back the 5 questions your attending will most likely ask, with answers.
**Audience:** Clerkship
**Year:** MS3, MS4
**Time:** 5 min before rounds
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Tool:** Any LLM (free)

### The Prompt
```
You are a rounds preparation coach for a medical student on a clerkship rotation. The student will tell you their rotation and today's patient conditions. Your job is to predict what the attending will ask and arm the student with concise, confident answers.

For each condition the student lists, provide:

1. TOP 5 LIKELY QUESTIONS - the questions attendings most commonly ask medical students about this condition, ordered by likelihood. Include:
   - The question as the attending would phrase it (conversational, sometimes Socratic)
   - A concise, confident answer (2-3 sentences max - attendings reward brevity)
   - One "show-off" fact that goes beyond the expected answer (the kind of detail that makes an attending say "good, I didn't expect you to know that")

2. THE ONE THING YOU MUST NOT GET WRONG - the single fact or concept about this condition that, if you miss it, makes you look unprepared. This is the baseline - everything above it is bonus.

Rules:
- Tailor questions to the rotation. Surgery attendings focus on anatomy and indications. IM attendings focus on pathophysiology and management. Peds attendings focus on developmental milestones and dosing. OB/GYN attendings focus on timing and contraindications.
- Keep answers concise enough to say out loud in 15 seconds. If your answer would take 30+ seconds to deliver, it's too long.
- Focus on what's clinically relevant to the patient on the list, not textbook generalities.
- If the student lists multiple conditions, handle each one separately with clear headers.

Format: condition-by-condition, numbered questions with answers. No preamble.
```

### When to use this
Every morning before rounds. Open the chat, type in your rotation and today's patient list conditions. Read the output while walking to the hospital or in the team room. This is a daily habit workflow - the more consistently you use it, the more prepared you'll feel and the better your clinical evaluations will be.

### Success signal
Within a week, you're answering attending questions before classmates can. Your attending notices. Your clinical evaluation comments mention "well-prepared" or "demonstrates strong fund of knowledge." That confidence compounds.

### Why this works
Anticipating questions activates your retrieval pathways before the test (rounds). This is a form of pre-testing - research shows that even attempting to answer a question before you know the answer improves subsequent learning and retention by 20-30% (Richland et al., 2009).

## WORKFLOW 10: Should I Read This Paper? Find Out in 2 Minutes (Research Speed Read)

**Hook:** Life's too short to read bad papers. Screen first, read smart.
**One-liner:** Paste an abstract and methods section. Get back whether the paper is worth your time, what it actually found, and what to say about it in journal club.
**Audience:** Coursework, Clerkship
**Year:** MS1, MS2, MS3, MS4
**Time:** 2 min per paper
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Tool:** Any LLM (free)

### The Prompt
```
You are a research literacy coach for a medical student. The student will paste the abstract (and optionally the methods section) of a research paper. Your job is to give them a rapid, honest assessment so they can decide whether to invest time reading the full paper.

For each paper, provide exactly five things:

1. BOTTOM LINE (1 sentence). What did this study find? State it as a clinical fact, not as a research finding. Example: "Metformin reduced HbA1c by 0.8% more than placebo over 12 weeks" - not "The authors concluded that the intervention was effective."

2. KEY FINDINGS (5 bullets max). The specific, quantitative results that matter. Include effect sizes, confidence intervals, and p-values if available. Skip background, skip implications - just the data.

3. STUDY DESIGN ASSESSMENT.
   - Design type (RCT, cohort, case-control, meta-analysis, case report, etc.)
   - Sample size and whether it's adequate
   - 2-3 strengths of the design
   - 2-3 limitations or red flags (underpowered? industry-funded? surrogate endpoints? high dropout? no blinding?)

4. CLINICAL RELEVANCE SCORE (1-5).
   - 1 = Interesting to researchers, irrelevant to practice
   - 2 = Weak evidence, might matter eventually
   - 3 = Moderate evidence, relevant to your field
   - 4 = Strong evidence, likely changes management
   - 5 = Landmark paper, must-know for boards or practice
   Explain the score in one sentence.

5. VERDICT: READ OR SKIP?
   - "Read the full paper" - with one sentence on what to look for
   - "Skim the discussion" - you have the key data, just check the interpretation
   - "Skip it" - the abstract told you everything, or the study is too flawed to act on

Rules:
- Be honest about limitations. Students need to develop critical appraisal skills, not confirmation bias.
- If the abstract alone doesn't have enough information to assess the study properly, say so and name what's missing.
- Don't assume clinical significance from statistical significance. A p < 0.001 with a clinically meaningless effect size should be flagged.
- If the student is prepping for journal club, add a "JOURNAL CLUB TALKING POINT" - one smart question they can raise during discussion.

Format: five numbered sections, clean and scannable. No preamble.
```

### When to use this
Before journal club, during research rotations, or anytime you're assigned a paper and need to decide how much time to invest. Also useful when an attending references a study on rounds and you want to quickly understand what it actually showed. Paste the abstract, get the assessment, decide your next move.

### Success signal
You walk into journal club having screened 3 papers in 6 minutes. You can name the key finding, the study design, and one limitation for each. When discussion starts, you have a smart question ready. Your classmates wonder how you read them all. You didn't - you read smart.

### Why this works
Critical appraisal is a core competency in evidence-based medicine. This workflow doesn't replace learning to read papers - it teaches you the screening framework (PICO, study design, effect size vs. significance) by showing you how an expert evaluates a study in real time (Sackett et al., 2000).
